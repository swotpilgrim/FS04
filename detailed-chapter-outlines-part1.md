## Chapter 1: Summer of Love

**Target Word Count:** 1,700 words
**POV:** Bex Morrison, first person (1967 flashback)
**Setting:** San Francisco, Haight-Ashbury commune and Golden Gate Park
**Time:** Summer evening, 1967

**Purpose:** Establish young Bex's idealism, her relationship with Marcus, and the 1967 counterculture world she must later resurrect as cover.

**Beats:**
1. Opening in commune kitchen, Bex (21) helping prepare communal dinner, surrounded by radical talk and revolutionary literature. Natural chameleon ability shown—she shifts between cooking, political discussion, comforting distressed runaway, all seamlessly.
2. Marcus Webb (24) arrives, charismatic philosophy grad student everyone calls "The Professor." He and Bex have been together six months. He's teaching her systemic critique, giving framework to her working-class rage. Their chemistry and intellectual connection established.
3. After dinner, demonstration planning meeting. Marcus speaks passionately about ending the war, dismantling oppressive systems. Bex watches him, believes completely. She's medical tent volunteer—mentions treating protesters injured by police. Her skills come from watching her mom patch up dad's mechanic shop accidents.
4. Golden Gate Park at sunset. Bex and Marcus walk through redwood groves, lie in meadow. Talk about building new world, genuine revolution. He tells her she sees things others miss, asks if she ever feels like different person depending on who she's with. She admits yes, doesn't know which version is real. He: "Maybe they're all real." They're in love, young, certain they'll change everything.

**Dialogue Topics:** Revolutionary theory accessible to working-class Bex, war resistance strategy, Marcus's philosophy background, commune dynamics, Bex's medical tent experiences, their future together building new society, her chameleon nature as gift not confusion.

**Emotional Arc:** Idealistic passion and certainty. Bex believes in revolution completely, loves Marcus deeply, feels purposeful for first time. No cynicism yet—pure 1967 optimism before everything collapsed.

**Key Details:** Commune Victorian matches safe house she'll return to in 1975. Marcus's teaching style and ideological framework. Bex's medical skills and chameleon adaptability. Golden Gate Park locations they'll revisit. Period details: anti-war posters, communal living, flower child aesthetics, genuine belief.

## Chapter 2: The Crash

**Target Word Count:** 1,700 words
**POV:** Bex Morrison, tight third person (1973)
**Setting:** Haight-Ashbury free clinic, then FBI approach location
**Time:** Late night, 1973

**Purpose:** Show Bex's collapse and FBI recruitment, establishing her desperation and vulnerability when PROJECT MIRROR approaches.

**Beats:**
1. Bex (27) at free clinic, third day of marathon shift helping overdose cases. Haight has collapsed—hard drugs replaced peace and love, violence everywhere, friends dying. She's exhausted, hollow, running on fumes. Helps young girl ODing on heroin, saves her life, girl runs away without thanks. The dream is dead.
2. Outside clinic at dawn, Bex smoking cigarette, contemplating leaving San Francisco with nowhere to go. FBI agents approach: Thomas Garrett (early 50s, ex-military, gruff) and partner. They've been watching her—noticed her chameleon ability, how she's trusted in multiple radical networks, can become whoever situation needs. "That's a gift. We can give you purpose for it."
3. Garrett explains PROJECT MIRROR: deep-cover infiltration program recruiting natural chameleons. Training, salary, structure, using her abilities to serve national security. Bex's initial resistance—she ran away from straight society, doesn't trust government. Garrett: "You're 27, broke, watching friends die. We're offering survival." He's not wrong.
4. Bex accepts. Has nothing left—Marcus disappeared from her life years ago (she left in 1968, breakdown she couldn't explain to him), friends are dead or gone, revolution failed. Maybe government service is purpose. Maybe she can start over. Garrett: "We'll train you to be anyone. Just don't lose yourself." She thinks she already has.

**Dialogue Topics:** Overdose treatment details, Haight's collapse and decay, FBI's awareness of her movements, PROJECT MIRROR program overview, Garrett's recruitment pitch focusing on survival not ideology, Bex's working-class pragmatism accepting offer, warnings about psychological risks.

**Emotional Arc:** Exhaustion and disillusionment to desperate acceptance. Bex has lost everything 1967 promised. Recruitment offers escape from failure, purpose from chaos. She's broken enough to accept, doesn't realize cost.

**Key Details:** Free clinic atmosphere (grim, overwhelmed, post-Summer of Love decay). Garrett's paternalistic recruitment style. Bex's physical deterioration from years of poverty and trauma. Her isolation—Marcus is gone, friends are casualties. 1973 setting: post-Vietnam, Watergate beginning, institutional collapse everywhere.

## Chapter 3: Breaking and Remaking

**Target Word Count:** 1,700 words
**POV:** Bex Morrison, tight third person (1973-1975 training)
**Setting:** CIA facility outside Sacramento
**Time:** Two-year training period, compressed montage

**Purpose:** Establish PROJECT MIRROR training methods, dangers, and Bex's compartmentalization strategy that will fail during mission.

**Beats:**
1. Training facility introduction. Sterile, institutional, isolated. Bex and dozen other recruits (con artists, actors, lifelong survivors—all chameleons). Instructors explain: deep-cover work requires becoming new person psychologically, not just performing. Some recruits will fracture permanently. Brutal honesty about risks.
2. Identity construction exercises. Build persona, live as them for weeks, snap back to baseline. Bex excels—can inhabit identities completely, thinks in character, becomes them. Watches other recruits struggle: some can't snap back, develop dissociative disorders, one requires psychiatric institutionalization after losing core self. Instructors warn constantly: "Go too deep and you don't come back."
3. Bex develops compartmentalization strategy: builds mental walls between identities, never lets them bleed. Becomes perfect operative—can be anyone, return to Bex cleanly. Graduates top of cohort. Garrett proud, instructors impressed. She thinks she's mastered the program's dangers.
4. Final briefing before deployment (1975). Instructors repeat warnings about maintaining boundaries, never building cover too close to real self. Bex confident in her compartmentalization. Garrett assigns her to San Francisco mission. She's ready. Thinks training worked. Doesn't realize it worked until it didn't.

**Dialogue Topics:** Training psychology and methodology, identity construction techniques, warnings about fracture risks, other recruits' struggles and failures, Bex's compartmentalization explanations, instructor concerns about her confidence, Garrett's pride in his recruit, mission deployment approval.

**Emotional Arc:** Determined competence masking underlying fragility. Bex rebuilds herself through training, becomes weapon, feels powerful and controlled. Doesn't recognize she's building house on sand—compartmentalization only works until mission requires resurrecting real self.

**Key Details:** Facility's institutional coldness. Other recruits as cautionary tales. Specific training exercises (method acting, behavioral psychology, identity immersion). Bex's false confidence. Two-year timeline compressed. Garrett's paternalistic oversight. 1975 deployment timing—eight years since Summer of Love, totally different era.

**Key Details:** FBI safe house's sterile professionalism vs. coming undercover chaos. Marcus's surveillance photos showing how he's aged. Bex's visceral reaction to seeing him. ## Chapter 5: Becoming Willow

**Target Word Count:** 1,700 words
**POV:** Bex Morrison, tight third person shifting toward first person
**Setting:** FBI safe house preparation room
**Time:** Three days of cover identity construction

**Purpose:** Show Bex building cover identity that's actually her past self, establishing core psychological trap.

**Beats:**
1. Bex constructs "Willow" cover identity: burned-out former radical returning to movement after years away. Creates backstory, mannerisms, beliefs. Realizes immediately: to sell this authentically to paranoid radicals, she can't fake the politics or culture. They'll test her knowledge, sense any performance. Must become who she actually was in 1967.
2. The trap becomes clear to Bex: PROJECT MIRROR trained compartmentalization between separate identities. But Willow isn't separate—she's 1967 Bex resurrected. Can't compartmentalize past from present when past IS the cover. She explains concern to Garrett. He acknowledges risk but orders her to proceed: "Use your real history as foundation. That's what makes you credible."
3. Physical transformation: grows out hair, adopts period clothing and style, sheds operative mannerisms, practices Willow's voice and movements. Studies radical theory she actually believed eight years ago. The transformation is effortless—too effortless. She's not building character, she's remembering who she was. Feels like peeling away Bex (operative) to reveal Bex (radical) underneath.
4. Final rehearsal with Garrett playing hostile radical testing her credentials. She responds perfectly—quotes theory, references demonstrations she actually attended, shows scars from police batons, speaks with genuine passion. Garrett: "That's incredible. You really believe it." Bex: "I did believe it. That's the problem." Garrett: "Use that. Just remember to come back when mission's done." She doesn't know if she can.

**Dialogue Topics:** Willow's backstory construction, radical credentials preparation, Bex's concerns about identity bleeding with past self, Garrett's operational calculus prioritizing mission over operative safety, period details Bex lived vs. researched, her genuine beliefs from 1967 vs. current cynicism, final rehearsal demonstrating authenticity.

**Emotional Arc:** Growing unease beneath professional competence. Bex recognizes she's being sent into psychological minefield—becoming who she was means risking loss of who she is. But training and loyalty to Garrett override self-preservation instincts.

**Key Details:** Cover identity documents and backstory details. Physical transformation matching 1967 appearance. Radical theory Bex genuinely believed vs. performing now. Garrett's awareness of danger but mission priority. Bex's compartmentalization already failing—can't wall off her own past. Setup for inevitable fracture.
